An 8-stage manufacturing journey, nobody had mapped end-to-end

Global Industrial Manufacturer · Connected Hardware-Software Products
Interactive workflow map — click stages, toggle the AI solution overlay
Open full screen ↗
Problem
  • 7 functional teams handled a B2B partner's operation between sales order and shopfloor delivery.
  • 8 process stages, each on a different tool, with manual handoffs at nearly every boundary.
  • Rework and delays were routine, but no one had ever mapped the full journey to see where.
Impact
  • 8 AI-pilot concepts scoped against the audited workflow.
  • 1 vendor pilot funded and progressing (OCR + LLM).
  • 7 paused before any budget committed — vendors couldn't define success against the brief.
Approach
  • Owned the audit end to end — the interviews, the workflow map, the pilot briefs, the vendor evaluations, the funding calls.
  • Ran qualitative research on the complete workflow as it actually happens — sitting with the people doing the work, not reading the process document.
  • Spent time with an expert on each team to understand their mission, their objectives, and the specific challenges they were navigating day to day.
  • Built the interactive map above, color-coded manual vs. digital — brought clarity to all friction points in one look, so the impact of a potential solution could be visualized better.
  • Designed a distinct AI intervention for each of the 8 stages — an AR-guided assembly tool and a remote AI quality inspection tool went furthest in vendor conversations.
Principle

Calculated bets upfront protect the budget downstream. A pilot without a success criterion isn't a pilot — it's a subscription to hope. And technology is a tool for the problem, not a reason to go looking for one.

Data Strategy · Reliability Engineering

Quality measured by guesswork, until it wasn't

Global Industrial Manufacturer · Channel Commissioning Software, 70+ Countries
How the offline-first telemetry loop worked
Problem
  • Quality was measured by support tickets — a lagging signal, and the platform's use was predominantly offline by design.
  • Platform ran in 70+ countries, serving 2,500+ enterprise customers.
  • No real baseline existed — every quarterly "improvement" claim was an assertion, not a measurement.
Impact
  • 165% YoY revenue growth, past €1M ARR in year one.
  • 20% MAU growth.
Approach

The platform was built for a predominantly offline, industrial use case — which made "measure everything" a harder problem than it sounds. Engineers flagged that a real telemetry solution wasn't a sprint-sized fix, and the idea sat benched longer than it should have.

I chose to run the marathon instead of waiting for a sprint-sized shortcut. Working with the team, we designed an offline-first architecture: data captured locally the moment it happened, synced to the server the moment the device reconnected.

  • Reliability — the user's probability of completing the task as intended.
  • Performance — the probability of completing it within a stipulated time.
  • Crash reports — user consent to send log files back, building a real dataset to troubleshoot from.

Every use case had to report all three, or it wasn't considered instrumented.

Principle

You can't improve what you can't measure.

Hardware × Software Integration

An industry-first switchboard, with no playbook to follow

Global Industrial Manufacturer · Connected Hardware + Cloud Software
3-layer architecture — data flowing product → gateway → software
Problem
  • Industry-first integration: circuit breakers, wireless sensors, and cloud dashboard talking natively over LoRaWAN. No playbook.
  • Inherited mid-flight: MVP scope set, launch date fixed, timeline slipping, 50+ features queued.
Impact
  • Shipped the industry's first natively-connected switchboard on the original date.
  • Cut 75% of the feature backlog to ensure timely delivery to market.
Approach

As Product Lead, I owned the complete system value across hardware and software — not just the documentation, the actual design decisions connecting circuit breaker, edge gateway, and energy management software into one coherent flow.

  • Cut 75% of the backlog against three criteria: no commercial path, out of MVP scope, or purely cosmetic — to ensure timely delivery to market.
  • Redirected 6 mobile developers off the configuration tool and onto other projects. There was no business case for polishing a tool further while the real value proposition — the connected system itself — was still under pilot test.
Principle

A just-enough product on a quick timeline beats a great product built over years. Speed to market is a feature nobody puts on the spec sheet, but customers feel it first.

0→1 Incubation

A business that didn't exist, funded by a reframed pitch

Global Industrial Manufacturer · Circular Economy Offer
The technical cycle — three loops back into the value chain, before the material ever hits landfill
Problem

Ambitions for sustainability were high — real impact against SDG targets and carbon neutrality. In practice, the harder problem was commercial: avoiding cannibalization of existing revenue streams, protecting profitability, and building a parallel system that could run as seamlessly as the open-loop manufacturing process it sat beside.

  • No supply process. No factory line. No pricing model. No budget.
  • Post-pandemic: every capital request scrutinized. Sustainability-led pitches had been refused twice — they read as a cost line, not a return.
Impact
  • The new business model created an incremental revenue stream, with net profitability up to 30% higher than the traditional offer.
  • World Economic Forum named the offer a Lighthouse Project.
Approach

Single point of orchestration — operating like a founder inside a large company, with no team handed to me. Every function that needed to touch this offer (Legal, Supply Chain, Industrialisation, Branding, Communications, Industrial Design, Sustainability, country sales) I had to convince, align, and coordinate individually.

Reframed the pitch. Prior refusals had led with "sustainability" as the value. I pivoted the narrative: a refurbished-product line is a financial hedge against supply-chain shocks. When raw materials inflate, a second inventory stream lets you keep shipping to customers at healthy margin. Sustainability was also made into sustaining business, sustaining profits, and sustaining the planet.

  • Won corporate budget in a 10-minute pitch to senior leadership after two refusals.
  • Scouted usable supply from recalls and cancelled orders. Ran a conjoint-analysis study with 100+ customers to set pricing and warranty terms.
  • Earned LCA certification (Bureau Veritas) myself to own the environmental case directly in customer conversations.
Principle

A big enough mission doesn't stop at a closed door — it checks for the window. Reframe the pitch, track inventory by hand if that's what it takes, do whatever the venture actually needs. Commercial success comes first; the system to support it gets built after.

Reliability × UX Design

Making a stuck screen feel fast, without a million-dollar fix

Global Industrial Manufacturer · Firmware Upgrade Use Case
Laptop commissioning tool, wired to the switchboard it configures
Problem
  • A specific use case had steep reliability and performance requirements. Root cause: sophisticated interoperability between the hardware connected and the software driving it.
  • In users' own words: "The screen loads for a long time once the action is initiated." "I'm stuck from doing other actions on the software until the firmware update is completed."
Impact
  • 30% improvement in performance metrics for the use case.
  • Enabled a workflow change so users could work on the software in parallel while a firmware upgrade was running.
Approach

Engineers proposed the technically correct fix: a multi-year, multi-million-dollar hardware platform investment. Not viable on any timeline that mattered to customers waiting today. To prioritize them anyway, we ideated a partial solution and were upfront that a permanent fix would come later.

The idea came from something I'd read: lift lobbies install mirrors not to make elevators faster, but to give people something to do while they wait — the wait feels shorter even though it isn't. A friendly deception. We built a spin-off installer that ran in parallel with the firmware upgrade. The upgrade still took just as long — but users could keep working on the software while it happened, so the wait stopped feeling like a wall.

Conventional product design says minimize clicks. In a hardware-software system, we found the opposite: fewer clicks meant longer unbroken waits, because the hardware still needed the same time to respond. We redesigned the flow into more micro-actions — more individual clicks, but each one gave the user visible progress and something active to do. More clicks, better experience.

Principle

Convention says minimize clicks. But when hardware sets the pace, more micro-actions beat one long wait — customer experience outranks convention, every time.
